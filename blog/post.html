The Zodiac Killer: America's Most Infamous Unsolved Case

By Michelle Lawson
April 15, 2025
42 Comments

In the late 1960s, a mysterious figure terrorized Northern California, murdering at least five people and taunting police with cryptic letters and ciphers. Despite one of the most intensive manhunts in history, the Zodiac Killer was never caught. More than 50 years later, the case continues to fascinate and perplex investigators and amateur sleuths alike.

The Confirmed Victims

The Zodiac Killer's confirmed victims were young couples in secluded areas, plus a lone taxi driver. The confirmed attacks include:

  • December 20, 1968: David Faraday (17) and Betty Lou Jensen (16) were shot and killed on Lake Herman Road in Vallejo.
  • July 4, 1969: Michael Mageau (19) and Darlene Ferrin (22) were shot at Blue Rock Springs Park in Vallejo. Ferrin died, but Mageau survived.
  • September 27, 1969: Bryan Hartnell (20) and Cecelia Shepard (22) were stabbed at Lake Berryessa in Napa County. Hartnell survived, but Shepard died.
  • October 11, 1969: Taxi driver Paul Stine (29) was shot in the Presidio Heights neighborhood of San Francisco.

The Letters and Ciphers

What made the Zodiac case particularly unusual was the killer's communication with the public. Between 1969 and 1974, he sent numerous letters to Bay Area newspapers, including the San Francisco Chronicle, the San Francisco Examiner, and the Vallejo Times-Herald.

Many of these letters included ciphers or coded messages. The first cipher was cracked in 1969 by a high school teacher and his wife. It began with the chilling words: "I LIKE KILLING PEOPLE BECAUSE IT IS SO MUCH FUN."

"I am not afraid of the gas chamber because it will send me to paradice [sic] all the sooner because I now have enough slaves to work for me." - The Zodiac Killer

A second cipher, known as the "340 cipher" (named for its 340 characters), remained unsolved for 51 years until it was finally cracked in December 2020 by a team of amateur code-breakers.

The Investigation

Despite one of the largest manhunts in U.S. history, the Zodiac Killer was never caught. Over the decades, investigators have considered more than 2,500 suspects. Among the most notable were:

  • Arthur Leigh Allen: A former elementary school teacher who was the prime suspect for many years. He was identified by survivor Michael Mageau in a photo lineup in 1991, 20 years after the shooting. Allen died in 1992 before he could be charged. DNA evidence later appeared to clear him.
  • Rick Marshall: A film projectionist who became a suspect when investigators noted similarities between his handwriting and the Zodiac letters.
  • Lawrence Kane: A businessman who was identified by Kathleen Johns, a woman who claimed to have been abducted by the Zodiac in 1970 (though this attack is not confirmed to be the work of the Zodiac).

The Legacy

The Zodiac case has had a profound impact on American popular culture, inspiring books, movies (including David Fincher's 2007 film "Zodiac"), TV shows, and countless amateur investigations. The case remains officially open in multiple jurisdictions.

In recent years, advances in forensic technology have offered new hope. In 2018, investigators used genetic genealogy to identify the Golden State Killer, another long-unidentified California serial killer. Similar techniques might one day identify the Zodiac.

Until then, the Zodiac remains one of America's most enduring criminal mysteries, a shadowy figure who killed at least five people, claimed to have killed many more, and then vanished, leaving behind a legacy of fear, fascination, and unanswered questions.
